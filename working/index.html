Understand pharmacy performance

The Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services (CMS) holds Medicare Advantage (Part C) health plans and Medicare Part D prescription drug plans accountable for low-cost, quality care. These plans are rated on their quality using “Star Ratings.” Star Ratings were created in 2006 as a way to report on plan quality and reward plans demonstrating high quality. Since Medicare Part D is a prescription benefit, and a portion of Medicare Part C plans often includes prescription benefits, there’s a big focus on how pharmacy teams can help these plans improve quality.

Medication-related quality measures

  • Medication Adherence for Diabetes Medications*
  • Medication Adherence for Hypertension (RAS Antagonists)*
  • Medication Adherence for Cholesterol (Statins)*
  • MTM Program Completion Rate for CMR
  • Statin Use in Person with Diabetes (current display measure, proposed for inclusion in Star Ratings for 2019)
Quality Measures and the Pharmacy Team’s Role (CE)

Value-based purchasing arrangements—in which providers are rewarded for demonstrating superior performance on accepted measures of quality care—are becoming increasingly more prevalent. Contracting strategies vary among health plans/PBMs, but many are taking creative strategies to tie quality performance to reimbursements. And, while not all performance criteria are tied to Star Ratings, one commonality you will find in these criteria is the emphasis on the medication adherence quality measures asterisked above.

2018 Performance Criteria by PBM chart

So what does it mean for a contract to have performance criteria?
Instead of a Medicare contract including straight reimbursement rates, many of the contracts now also include certain performance criteria a pharmacy must meet in order to avoid/reduce Direct and Indirect Remuneration (DIR) fees or receive a potential DIR rebate. Each year, we publish a Medicare 101 guide that details each major Medicare contract and its rates, performance criteria, DIR fee measurements and calculations, etc.

Tackle medication adherence

In order to improve medication adherence, pharmacies must focus on patient education coupled with workflow tools. Begin by assessing what patient education tools and techniques you are utilizing in your pharmacy and then review what workflow tools would work based on your patients’ barriers to adherence. Come up with your own action plan to improve adherence both with patient education and workflow management.

Medication Therapy Management (MTM)

Pharmacists provide MTM to help patients get the best benefits from their medications by actively managing drug therapy and by identifying, preventing and resolving medication-related problems.
